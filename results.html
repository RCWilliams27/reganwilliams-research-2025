Topological Structure

We explored how graphs encode shape and structure through algebraic tools like the Euler characteristic and the even and odd Laplacians.

See Key Results
  • Euler Characteristic: Defined \( \chi(\Gamma) = |V| - |E| \), a fundamental topological invariant for graphs. Interpreted this as \( \chi = b_0 - b_1 \), where \( b_0 \) is the number of connected components and \( b_1 \) the number of independent cycles.
  • Gluing Formula: When graphs \( \Gamma_1 \) and \( \Gamma_2 \) are joined along a shared subgraph \( B \), we proved the identity: \[\chi(\Gamma_1 \cup \Gamma_2) = \chi(\Gamma_1) + \chi(\Gamma_2) - \chi(B)\]
  • Even and Odd Laplacians: Constructed the even Laplacian \( \Delta_+ = II^\top \) and odd Laplacian \( \Delta_- = I^\top I \) using the incidence matrix \( I \). Showed that: \[\dim(\ker(\Delta_+)) = b_0, \quad \dim(\ker(\Delta_-)) = b_1\] revealing how the Laplacian spectrum encodes topology.
  • Morse Inequalities and Discrete Morse Theory: Used critical point counts from Morse functions on graphs to verify: \[ C_0 - C_1 = b_0 - b_1 = \chi(\Gamma) \]

Entropy

We investigated the von Neumann entropy of graphs as a measure of structural complexity, based on the spectral properties of the Laplacian. This entropy encodes randomness, symmetry, and connectivity, and serves as a bridge between combinatorics, quantum information, and network theory. We analyzed how entropy behaves under gluing operations and disjoint unions, and how it reflects global structural features.

See Key Results
  • Von Neumann Entropy: Defined entropy as \[ S(\Gamma) = \sum_{i} \lambda_i \ln \lambda_i, \] where \( \lambda_i \) are the nonzero eigenvalues of the Laplacian.
  • Graph Family Comparisons: Computed and compared entropy for key graph types: path, tree, cycle, star, lattice, torus, complete bipartite, and complete graphs. We found that entropy increases with size and symmetry; that is, complete graphs have higher entropy than trees or paths.
  • Entropy Additivity: Verified that entropy is additive under disjoint unions: \[ S(\Gamma_1 \cup \Gamma_2) = S(\Gamma_1) + S(\Gamma_2), \] and used this to compare composite and individual systems.
  • Gluing and Entropy: Explored entropy behavior under gluing via two methods:
    • Bridge Gluing: Connecting two graphs by adding an edge between selected vertices
    • Interface Gluing: Identifying a shared subgraph between two graphs
    In both cases, we studied how entropy is no longer purely additive and instead reflects the new structure.
